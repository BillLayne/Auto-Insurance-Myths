Red Cars Cost More to Insure? The Truth Behind NC's Most Popular Insurance Myth

We're hitting the brakes on this common misconception and revealing the real factors that drive up your auto insurance rates in North Carolina.

By Bill Layne | Last Updated: July 17, 2025

Key Takeaways

  • Car color does NOT affect insurance rates. Insurers in North Carolina don't ask for your car's color when you get a quote.
  • The real factors are about risk. Your rate is determined by your car's make/model, your driving record, your location, and more.
  • The myth comes from psychology. Red is associated with speed and risk, but this doesn't translate to insurance calculations.
  • Focus on what you can control. You can lower your premium by being a safe driver, choosing a safe car, and comparing quotes.

Does car color affect insurance premiums?

No, the color of your car does not affect your insurance premiums in North Carolina or anywhere else. Auto insurance companies calculate your rate based on risk factors like your vehicle's make and model, your driving history, your location, and your coverage choices—not its paint job. The idea that a red car costs more to insure is a persistent myth with no basis in how insurance policies are actually priced.

It's one of the most enduring myths we hear from clients in Elkin, Mount Airy, and across the Yadkin Valley: "I'd love a red car, but I hear they're a fortune to insure." Let's be crystal clear: this is completely false. When you call us for a quote, we'll ask for your Vehicle Identification Number (VIN), which tells us everything we need to know about your car—its make, model, year, engine size, and safety features. What it doesn't tell us? The color. And frankly, we don't need to know.

So, what actually determines my car insurance rate in NC?

Your car insurance rate in North Carolina is determined by a combination of factors related to you, your vehicle, and your coverage. Key elements include your driving record, age, credit history, the make and model of your car (including its safety ratings and repair costs), how much you drive, and where you live. The types and amounts of coverage you select, along with your deductible, also play a major role in the final cost.

Insurers are in the business of assessing risk. Here’s a breakdown of what they actually look at:

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Do red cars get more speeding tickets?

There is no conclusive evidence that red cars get more speeding tickets. While a bright red car might be more visible to law enforcement, police officers target drivers based on their speed and driving behavior, not the color of their vehicle. The idea that red cars are ticket magnets is another persistent but unproven myth.

What is the cheapest color car to insure?

There is no single 'cheapest' car color for insurance. Insurers do not use car color as a rating factor. Instead, factors like the car's make, model, safety features, repair costs, and theft rate have a much greater impact on your insurance premium. A white sedan will likely have the same base insurance rate as an identical red one.

How can I lower my car insurance in North Carolina?

To lower your car insurance in NC, you can maintain a good driving record, bundle your auto and home policies, increase your deductible, ask for discounts (like good student or safe driver), and improve your credit score. Shopping around and comparing quotes from different insurers, like Bill Layne Insurance Agency, is also a key strategy.
